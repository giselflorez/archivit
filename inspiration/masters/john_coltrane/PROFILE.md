# JOHN COLTRANE
## NORTHSTAR MASTER PROFILE

**Full Name:** John William Coltrane
**Born:** September 23, 1926, Hamlet, North Carolina
**Died:** July 17, 1967, Huntington, New York
**Disciplines:** Jazz Saxophone, Composition, Improvisation, Spiritual Music

---

## GLOBAL UNDERSTANDING

### Biography

John Coltrane was an American jazz saxophonist, bandleader, and composer. He is among the most influential and acclaimed figures in jazz history, and one of the most important musicians of the 20th century. Coltrane's work explored the outer limits of jazz improvisation, developed new techniques that expanded the vocabulary of the instrument, and infused his music with deep spiritual meaning.

He emerged through the bands of Dizzy Gillespie, Miles Davis, and Thelonious Monk before forming his own classic quartet with McCoy Tyner, Jimmy Garrison, and Elvin Jones. His career, though tragically short (he died at 40), produced a body of work that continues to influence musicians across all genres.

### NORTHSTAR

**SPIRITUAL TRANSCENDENCE THROUGH SOUND** - Coltrane's NORTHSTAR was the pursuit of music as a direct pathway to God. After a spiritual awakening in 1957, he dedicated his life to creating music that could "speak to souls" and "help humanity free itself from its hangups." His mission was nothing less than using sound to connect humans to the divine.

### Core Belief

> "I want to be a force for real good. In other words, I know there are bad forces. I want to be the opposite force. I want to be the force which is truly for good."

Coltrane believed music was a sacred practice - a form of prayer that could elevate both player and listener to higher states of consciousness.

## CHARACTER & MINDSET

### How He Thought

Coltrane approached music as **spiritual practice**, not entertainment. He practiced obsessively - often 12+ hours a day - viewing the saxophone as a tool for spiritual exploration. His mindset combined:

- **Relentless Self-Improvement**: Never satisfied, always seeking the next level
- **Spiritual Seeking**: Studied religions across traditions (Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Kabbalah)
- **Mathematical Exploration**: Investigated the geometric and mathematical structures underlying music
- **Ego Dissolution**: Sought to remove "self" from the music, becoming a vessel for divine expression

**Key Mental Frameworks:**
- **The Clean Mirror**: Constantly purify technique and intention to reflect truth more clearly
- **Universal Harmony**: All music, all religions, all people are expressions of one divine source
- **Vertical Exploration**: Go deeper into each chord, each note, each moment - exhaust all possibilities
- **Surrender to Sound**: Let the music play through you rather than by you

### Key Concepts

1. **Sheets of Sound**: The revolutionary technique of playing extremely dense, rapid arpeggios and scale patterns that critic Ira Gitler described as "sheets of sound" - hundreds of notes cascading like waterfalls.

2. **A Love Supreme**: His 1964 masterpiece, a four-part spiritual suite that serves as musical prayer. The work represents the apex of his devotional approach.

3. **Modal Exploration**: Moving beyond chord-based improvisation to explore modes (scales) as the basis for extended improvisation, pioneered with Miles Davis on "Kind of Blue."

4. **Free Jazz Embrace**: Later career exploration of "free jazz" and avant-garde forms, pushing beyond all conventional structures.

### His Process

- **Obsessive Practice**: Practiced until his lips bled, falling asleep with saxophone in hand
- **Multi-Phonics**: Developed techniques to play multiple notes simultaneously
- **Circular Breathing**: Extended techniques allowing continuous sound without breathing breaks
- **Collective Improvisation**: Created environments where all quartet members improvised simultaneously
- **Meditative Preparation**: Approached recording sessions as spiritual ceremonies
- **One-Take Authenticity**: "A Love Supreme" was recorded in a single session on December 9, 1964

## FINE-GRANULAR UNDERSTANDING

### Specific Techniques

**Sheets of Sound:**
- Extremely dense, rapid-fire arpeggios and scale patterns
- Playing "vertically" through all chord tones and extensions
- Up to hundreds of notes per phrase
- Exploring every harmonic implication within each chord
- Named by critic Ira Gitler in liner notes to "Soultrane" (1958)

Technical explanation: Coltrane would arpeggiate through multiple chord substitutions at extreme speed, essentially playing all possible notes that could relate to a chord, creating a continuous cascade of sound rather than discrete melodic phrases.

> "I started experimenting because I was striving for a more individual development. I even tried long, rapid lines that Ira Gitler termed 'sheets of sound' at that time."
> — John Coltrane

**Coltrane Changes (Giant Steps Progression):**
- Harmonic system dividing the octave into three equal parts
- Chord progressions moving by major thirds
- Creates symmetrical patterns challenging traditional improvisation
- "Giant Steps" remains a technical benchmark for saxophonists

**Three-on-One Chord Approach:**
- Arpeggiating three chords simultaneously over one chord
- Learned from Thelonious Monk
- Creates fluid, sweeping harmonic movement

**Multi-Phonics:**
- Playing multiple notes simultaneously through alternate fingerings
- Extended technique that became more prominent in late period

### Methods

**The Cleaning of the Mirror:**
> "There is never any end. There are always new sounds to imagine; new feelings to get at. And always, there is the need to keep purifying these feelings and sounds so that we can really see what we've discovered in its pure state. So that we can see more and more clearly what we are. In that way, we can give to those who listen the essence, the best of what we are. But to do that at each stage, we have to keep on cleaning the mirror."

**Collective Composition:**
The "A Love Supreme" session had no written music - only a structural chart. Musicians followed Coltrane's directions, "most of which were not spoken, but came from what they heard him playing." They were collectively composing by improvising together.

**The Psalm Method:**
In "Psalm," Coltrane plays his saxophone as a direct recitation of the poem he wrote. Each phrase of the horn corresponds to a phrase of the text - making it literally a "musical recitation of prayer by horn."

### Discoveries

- **Modal Jazz Development**: With Miles Davis, helped pioneer modal improvisation as alternative to bebop chord changes
- **Extended Form**: Expanded jazz composition beyond standard 32-bar forms to suite-length works
- **Spiritual Jazz Genre**: Essentially created the "spiritual jazz" subgenre
- **Soprano Saxophone Revival**: Brought the soprano saxophone back to prominence in jazz

## APPLICATION TO CRE-8

### Principles for Transcendent Creation

1. **THE SHEETS OF SOUND PRINCIPLE**: Explore every possibility within each moment. Don't just skim the surface - go vertical. Exhaust all options, all variations, all implications before moving on.

2. **PRACTICE AS PRAYER**: Your creative practice is not preparation for creation - it IS creation. It IS spiritual practice. Treat every session as ceremony.

3. **THE CLEAN MIRROR**: Constantly purify your technique and intention. Remove obstacles between you and pure expression. The clearer the vessel, the clearer the transmission.

4. **COLLECTIVE INTELLIGENCE**: True creation happens when individuals transcend ego and respond to each other in real-time. The group creates what no individual could alone.

5. **ONE-SESSION TRUTH**: "A Love Supreme" was recorded in one session. Trust the moment. Trust the preparation. When conditions are right, capture everything in one take.

6. **TRANSCEND CATEGORIES**: "Jazz is a word they use to sell our music." Don't let labels limit your exploration. The work defines itself.

7. **FORCE FOR GOOD**: Know what you're creating for. Coltrane wanted to be a "force for real good" - to counter the bad forces in the world. Your art should serve something larger than yourself.

### CRE-8 Implementation

- **Deep Practice Sessions**: Dedicated time for technical development as spiritual practice
- **Vertical Exploration**: For each project, exhaust all possibilities before moving to the next
- **Intention Setting**: Begin creative sessions with clear spiritual/ethical intention
- **Collective Improvisation**: Create frameworks for real-time collaborative creation
- **Documentation of Process**: Like the Smithsonian manuscript, preserve the notes, sketches, and artifacts of creation
